Fermentation is a natural process that converts sugars in food into acids, gases, or alcohol, using microorganisms such as bacteria, yeasts, or fungi. In the context of tomato preservation, fermentation can be used to create flavorful and tangy fermented tomato products such as lacto-fermented salsa or fermented tomato sauce. Here's how to ferment tomatoes:

1. Select Fresh Tomatoes:

Choose ripe, firm tomatoes with no signs of spoilage or damage. Cherry or grape tomatoes work well for fermentation due to their small size and firm texture.

2. Prepare Tomatoes:

  • Wash the tomatoes thoroughly under cold running water to remove any dirt or debris.
  • Remove stems and any blemishes or bruises from the tomatoes.
  • Cut the tomatoes into halves, quarters, or slices, depending on your preference and the intended use of the fermented tomatoes.

3. Prepare Brine Solution (Optional):

Prepare a brine solution by dissolving salt in water. The recommended ratio is typically 1-2 tablespoons of salt per quart of water. Use non-iodized salt, such as sea salt or pickling salt, to avoid undesirable flavors.

4. Pack Tomatoes in Fermentation Container:

  • Pack the prepared tomatoes tightly into a clean, sterilized fermentation vessel or jar, leaving some space at the top to allow for expansion during fermentation.
  • If using a brine solution, pour the prepared brine over the tomatoes, making sure they are fully submerged. Use a fermentation weight or a clean, food-safe object to keep the tomatoes submerged beneath the brine.

5. Fermentation Process:

  • Cover the fermentation vessel with a lid or a clean cloth to prevent dust and insects from entering while allowing gases to escape.
  • Place the fermentation vessel in a cool, dark place, ideally at room temperature (around 65-75°F or 18-24°C).
  • Ferment the tomatoes for several days to several weeks, depending on your desired level of fermentation and flavor development. Taste the tomatoes periodically to monitor the fermentation process.

6. Skim and Check for Mold (Optional):

  • During fermentation, you may notice foam or bubbles forming on the surface of the brine. This is a normal part of the fermentation process and can be skimmed off with a clean spoon if desired.
  • Occasionally check the tomatoes for signs of mold or spoilage. If you see any mold growth or notice an off smell, discard the batch and start over.

7. Taste and Store:

  • Once the tomatoes have reached your desired level of fermentation and developed a tangy flavor, they are ready to be consumed or stored.
  • Transfer the fermented tomatoes and brine to clean, airtight containers and store them in the refrigerator to slow down the fermentation process and preserve their flavor.
  • Fermented tomatoes can be enjoyed as a condiment, added to salads or sandwiches, or used in recipes such as salsas, sauces, or relishes.

By fermenting tomatoes, you can enhance their flavor and nutritional value while preserving them for extended periods. Experiment with different seasonings and spices to create unique fermented tomato products that suit your taste preferences. Remember to use proper sanitation and food safety practices to prevent the risk of foodborne illness during fermentation.
